# Quiet Proof Loops — style guide

> A calm six-second product proof: begin with one honest state, reveal one
> concrete capability through causal motion, hold long enough to verify it,
> then return exactly.

Quiet Proof Loops are proof instruments, not miniature advertisements. A loop
earns attention by making one shipped behavior inspectable. The viewer should
be able to name what changed, why it changed, and which qualifier limits the
claim without reading supporting copy.

## The decision system

Every episode answers seven questions in its `BRIEF.md` front matter:

1. What single fact are we proving?
2. Where is that fact verified in the current product?
3. What is the honest opening state?
4. What is the visible payoff?
5. What qualifier must remain attached?
6. Which one motion verb explains the transformation?
7. What is the highest review uncertainty?

If any answer is vague, the episode is not ready to animate. A palette-complete
clip with an unverified claim still fails.

## Story grammar

The opening state is honest, useful, and fully readable at frame zero. It may
be empty, unavailable, neutral, or unresolved when that is the product truth.
The hook names the proof rather than promising an outcome.

One causal transformation begins at the profile's motion marker. Motion must
explain the state change using exactly one primary verb:

- `expand`: reveal more of the same object or hierarchy;
- `activate`: turn a dormant capability on;
- `resolve`: replace uncertainty with a concrete product state;
- `flow`: carry cause or history through a visible chain;
- `cycle`: show a finite set of product states in place.

The payoff completes early enough to read. It then holds without ambient
distraction. The return retraces the state and ends on the exact opening frame;
the seam is a binary acceptance criterion, not a visual preference.

## Feeling and hierarchy

The SleepRadar profile should feel like a compact Home Assistant night readout:
calm, technical, low-glare, and candid. The hook is the first hierarchy level;
the product-native proof surface is second; qualifiers stay attached to the
state they limit. Semantic accents explain meaning. They do not decorate empty
space.

Prefer cards, entities, states, chart topology, and automation chains that a
Home Assistant user recognizes. Keep camera position fixed. Let the product
state do the storytelling.

## Locked, flexible, never

Locked by `frame.md`: output dimensions, duration, silence, typography,
palette, hook geometry, core card geometry, timing markers, output budgets,
loop thresholds, and forbidden treatments. Locked by the episode brief: one
claim, its evidence, visible qualifiers, opening state, payoff state, and one
motion verb.

Flexible per episode: feature subject, card topology, semantic accent,
signature motion, starting and payoff content, and the precise product-native
layout.

Never use decorative gradients, invented telemetry, medical or safety
language, generic wellness imagery, random motion, persistent shimmer, camera
theatrics, or a previous layout copied without feature-specific reasoning.

## Proof over promise

Good: a card moves from out-of-bed dashes to the shipped live fixture while
the stage remains labelled as an estimate.

Good: a trigger, condition, comfort automation, and response resolve in causal
order.

Bad: a radar glow pulses behind a claim without changing a product state.

Bad: a chart displays plausible-looking personal values that are not a public
fixture or clearly labelled illustration.

Bad: the qualifier appears only in a caption outside the proof surface.

## Review test

At native width, a reviewer should be able to answer four questions from the
clip alone: What fact is claimed? What state changed? What caused it? What
qualifier limits it? Failure on any answer is a claim, hierarchy, motion, or
readability redo—not a request for more decoration.
